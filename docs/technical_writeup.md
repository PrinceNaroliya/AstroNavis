# AstroNavis Technical Design Document v0.1

## Introduction

AstroNavis is an orbital intelligence and collision-avoidance platform designed to monitor space objects, predict conjunction events, evaluate collision risks, and analyze avoidance maneuvers.

The system combines orbital propagation, relative motion analysis, maneuver generation, and future risk evaluation to support safer satellite operations in increasingly congested orbital environments.

Rather than functioning as an autonomous control system, AstroNavis is intended as a decision-support platform that helps operators understand potential risks, compare maneuver strategies, and make informed operational decisions.

---

## Motivation

The rapid growth of satellite constellations, commercial space missions, and orbital debris has significantly increased congestion in Earth orbit.

As the number of active space objects continues to rise, conjunction events are becoming more frequent, creating operational and safety challenges for satellite operators. Even a single collision can generate thousands of debris fragments, threatening nearby spacecraft and long-term orbital sustainability.

Effective collision avoidance requires more than simply identifying a potential conjunction. Operators must understand relative motion, evaluate maneuver options, estimate operational impact, and assess whether a proposed maneuver could introduce new risks elsewhere in the orbital environment.

AstroNavis is being developed to address these challenges by providing an orbital intelligence and decision-support platform capable of conjunction analysis, maneuver evaluation, and future secondary collision risk assessment for increasingly complex space operations.

## Mathematical Foundations

### Position Vector

The position vector represents the satellite location in three-dimensional Earth-centered coordinates.

```text
r = (x, y, z)
```

### Velocity Vector

The velocity vector describes both the speed and direction of satellite motion.

```text
v = (vx, vy, vz)
```

### Relative Position

The relative position between two satellites is calculated as:

```text
rr = r₂ − r₁
```

This vector represents the displacement between two space objects.

### Relative Velocity

The relative velocity between two satellites is calculated as:

```text
rv = v₂ − v₁
```

This indicates how quickly the distance between satellites is changing.

### Dot Product Analysis

The dot product of relative position and relative velocity vectors is used to determine whether satellites are approaching or separating.

```text
dot(rr, rv)
```

**Interpretation**

* dot < 0 → Approaching
* dot > 0 → Separating
* dot ≈ 0 → Closest approach region

### RTN Coordinate Frame

AstroNavis uses the RTN orbital reference frame:

* **R (Radial)** → Away from Earth center
* **T (Tangential)** → Along orbital motion
* **N (Normal)** → Perpendicular to orbital plane

This frame simplifies maneuver generation and orbital reasoning.

### Delta-V Maneuvers

Candidate maneuvers are generated by applying a velocity change:

```text
v_new = v + Δv
```

Different maneuver directions can be evaluated using the RTN frame.

## Future Research Direction

One of the primary research directions of AstroNavis is secondary collision risk assessment.

Most existing collision avoidance approaches focus on avoiding a single conjunction event. However, a maneuver that prevents one collision may unintentionally increase the risk of future conjunctions with other nearby objects.

AstroNavis aims to investigate methods for evaluating maneuver safety not only for the immediate threat but also for the surrounding orbital environment. This capability could support more reliable and sustainable space traffic management in increasingly crowded Earth orbits.

Future development will focus on improving maneuver evaluation, fuel optimization, collision probability estimation, and secondary collision assessment capabilities.
